7 Things You Must Know Before Buying a DNA Kit on Amazon

Most people now buy DNA test kits directly from Amazon – often from brands like MyHeritage DNA, AncestryDNA, FamilyTreeDNA and LifeDNA. But when you send off a sample, you’re not just buying a product – you’re sharing your most personal data.

This guide walks you through the 7 most important safety and privacy questions you should understand in 2025 before you click “Buy now”.

1. Who really controls your DNA data?

All companies say “you own your DNA”. But what they can legally do with your data is very different from brand to brand.

  • MyHeritage DNA: Strong privacy stance, no police access, and good protections under EU/Israel privacy laws.
  • AncestryDNA: Does not sell your data, but may cooperate with law enforcement under a valid court order.
  • FamilyTreeDNA: Offers advanced tools; historically more open to law enforcement in specific cases.
  • LifeDNA: Focuses mostly on wellness and lifestyle. Always read their terms before uploading or testing.

3. Cybersecurity: how safe is your data from hackers?

Any large DNA database can become a target. Before buying, ask yourself:

  • Does the company publish clear security and breach policies?
  • Do they limit how your data can be accessed or downloaded?
  • Have they had breaches in the past – and how did they respond?

MyHeritage has previously reported a breach that affected login data, but not DNA; they reacted transparently and improved security. Larger ecosystems (like Ancestry and others) have more users, which can make any potential breach more impactful.

4. What happens to your physical DNA sample?

DNA kits leave a physical sample in a lab. That sample is either stored or destroyed after analysis:

  • Some companies destroy samples automatically after a period of time.
  • Others store your sample long-term unless you manually request destruction.
  • Many brands let you control this in your account settings – but the default is not always in your favour.

Before you order any kit on Amazon, check what the brand says about sample storage and destruction.

5. Your results also reveal information about your family

When you test, you’re not just exposing your own data. Close relatives (siblings, parents, children) become easier to identify too.

  • Large match databases (MyHeritage DNA, AncestryDNA, FamilyTreeDNA) can reveal unknown relatives.
  • This can be positive (connecting with family) or emotionally difficult (unexpected parents, half-siblings, or heritage).
  • Think about how you would feel if your results revealed something your family never talked about.

6. Are health and wellness reports solid science?

Not all kits focus on ancestry. Some – like LifeDNA – lean more into health, wellness and lifestyle guidance.

  • These reports can be interesting, but they are not medical diagnoses.
  • Your genetics are only one part of the picture; environment and lifestyle still matter a lot.
  • You should always discuss any serious health concerns with a qualified doctor, not just a DNA report.

7. The consent settings you must change after purchase

Once your kit arrives and you create an account, you’ll usually be asked to “help research” or “improve the product” by sharing data.

  • Look for toggles about research, third-party sharing and marketing.
  • Decide whether you want your anonymised data used for research or partnerships.
  • Review options for sample storage, DNA matching and profile visibility.

Spending five minutes on these settings often matters more for your long-term privacy than which kit you choose.

After you’ve checked the safety boxes, which kit should you buy?

If you want a good balance of price, ancestry detail and relative matching on Amazon, MyHeritage DNA is usually the easiest place to start. If your main goal is to build a big family tree, AncestryDNA is still the leader.
